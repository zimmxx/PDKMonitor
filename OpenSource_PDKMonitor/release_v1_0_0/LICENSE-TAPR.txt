PREAMBLE

Open Hardware is a thing -- a physical artifact, either electrical or
mechanical -- whose design information is available to, and usable by,
the public in a way that allows anyone to make, modify, distribute, and
use that thing.  In this preface, design information is called
"documentation" and things created from it are called "products."

The TAPR Open Hardware License ("OHL") agreement provides a framework
for hardware projects that are similar in spirit to those for the GNU
General Public License (GPL), but adapted to the needs of hardware
projects.  As is the case with the GPL, derivatives of an OHL design
must also be released under the OHL.

The OHL is not primarily a copyright license.  While copyright protects
documentation from unauthorized copying, modification, and
distribution, it has little to do with your right to make, distribute,
or use a product based on that documentation.  For better or worse,
patents play a significant role in those activities.  Whether or not
specifically mentioned in its text, every license must grapple with
patents, and the OHL is no exception.  Consequently, you may find
yourself making different kinds of promises to those who provide you
with OHL documentation than you would make for ordinary software.

Despite these differences, the OHL is closer in spirit to the GPL than
it is to any common hardware license.  Among other things, those who
benefit from an OHL design may not bring lawsuits claiming that design
infringes their patents or other intellectual property.

The OHL is not yet approved by the Open Source Initiative or endorsed
by the Free Software Foundation.  Neither organization is, as of this
writing, taking the position that hardware can be open source in the
same sense as software.  However, as you will see, the OHL captures the
spirit of open source as well as can be done given the realities of the
hardware world.

A frequently asked question (FAQ) document about the OHL is available.
You should read the FAQ even if you are familiar with software licenses
like the GPL.  Although it is meant to help you understand the OHL,
information in the FAQ is not a part of the OHL and has no role in
interpreting it.  In case of any conflict between the OHL and the FAQ,
the language of the OHL controls.
